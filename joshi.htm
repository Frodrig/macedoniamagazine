Interview with Josh Mandel

You may read the spanish translation of the interview
with additional notes about Josh Mandel's adventure games.

M.M.- Can you tell us how did you get involved in interactive entertainment?

J.M.- I had loved computer games for many years, ever since I had played the original Colossal Caves and the early Star Trek game on a mainframe (this was before computers had monitors; we typed our input on a terminal, and then the computer's responses were printed out in hard copy).

During the early 80's, I was a console gamer, and played mostly Colecovision games. In the mid-80's, I started to earn my gaming money by reviewing games for a magazine called Videogames And Computer Entertainment. I didn't buy my first computer until 1986, and I immediately began writing game reviews for the magazine and for CompuServe's online gaming magazine. (I didn't get paid for those reviews, but I got all the free games I could play!). Online, I met a fellow named Guruka Singh Khalsa, and he had read some of my reviews and walkthrus. When I found out that Guruka was the Producer at Sierra On-Line, I asked if he would allow me to be a beta-tester, and he said yes. I also beta-tested for Infocom at that time.

Beta-testing for Sierra was a great experience. I remember trying to track down a particularly elusive bug in Codename: Iceman, in which I was on the telephone with Mark Hood for at least a couple of hours while we figured out where the problem was. I would prepare very detailed beta reports and was, I think, a good tester for them.

So eventually, in a year or two, Guruka called one day to ask whether or not I'd be interested in working for Sierra on-site as his Assistant Producer, because the number of titles, and their scale, was getting too large for one Producer to handle. I was working in advertising at that time, and never thought I would take the job. But after visiting Sierra, meeting the people there (especially Guruka, Bill Davis, and Ken Williams), and seeing how beautiful Oakhurst was, I decided to take the job.

For the first 8 months, I was the Assistant Producer there, but I was able to move quickly to the Product Design department and begun writing and designing.

M.M.- You gave your voice to a number of characters on early CDROM games from Sierra, including Graham in King's Quest V. Your performance was one of the bests in that game; did you have any prior experience with voice acting?

J.M.- Thank you! I did have quite a bit of prior experience in voice acting before going to Sierra. I had been in a movie, on several television programs, and did voices for radio programs and commercials. My major in college was Acting and I had worked as a stand-up comedian for many years before going into advertising.

I was not very happy with my performance in King's Quest V. It was the first time Roberta had directed (although she had Mark Seibert to help her) and she wanted King Graham to always be "buff" and brave. There were many times in the recording where I would try to do some real acting, and give Graham moods and emotions, but Roberta kept saying, "He's never tired, he's King Graham!" or "He's never worried, he's King Graham!". So I felt the performance did not have enough emotion in it, but people seemed to like it anyway, so maybe Roberta was on to something.

M.M.- At that time, Sierra designers used to include several design tricks in games, like multiples ways of dying and dead ends. For instance, many people thinks that the dead-ends in the final chase of Laura Bow in The Dagger of Amon Ra are totally unfair. What was the designer intention with those dead ends?

J.M.- I don't want to speak for Bruce Balfour (the designer of Laura Bow 2), but I believe that he was trying to create a sense of great threat to Laura in that final act. In the earlier acts, Laura herself was never in jeopardy; it was always somebody else. (I think it was the same way in Laura Bow 1, The Colonel's Bequest.) And Laura Bow 2 was designed before it became out of fashion to have the main character die. After all, Roger Wilco died all kinds of gruesome deaths in the Space Quest series, and the fans seemed to love that.

The funny thing is that the last act of Roberta's game Phantasmagoria is exactly the same as the last act of Laura Bow 2; they both consist of a sudden "chase scene" with many, many dead ends and horrible deaths for the main character. And Phantasmagoria was designed years after Laura Bow 2. So I have to think that Roberta really liked the way Bruce designed the end of Laura Bow 2.

M.M.- Back to Dagger of Amon Ra, other criticism is that there are not enough clues to really find out the answer to all the questions you are faced at the end of the game. Are we players too dumb, or was the game a bit too hard?

J.M.- I think there were two problems there. One was that the player was not warned ahead of time that it was best to take notes. So most players got to the questions at the end of the game and said, "WHAT? I don't remember all of this!" and resented the fact that they had not been told earlier to make notes.

The other problem was that Bruce, like most of us, were trying to design games where there were more details than people might find the first time through the game. We wanted to increase playability, so we put in so much detail that people might need to play two or three times to find all the details. If the player could not answer some questions at the end, it was probably not because the player was dumb NOR because the game was too hard, but because there were so many details, a player was liable to miss some of them during the course of the game. And people like to play quickly, getting from one puzzle to the next as quickly as possible, so they would be even more likely to miss some details.

M.M.- You worked with Al Lowe in Freddy Pharkas: Frontier Pharmacist. How it was working with such a famous designer? What were your contributions to the game?

J.M.- It was great to work with Al. He let me do all sorts of things that he didn't necessarily want to do, and fortunately they turned out well. He designed most of the puzzles, but I took a few out (there were too many puzzles dealing with "bathroom humor") and added quite a few others. For instance, his design only had three acts; I added a fourth act. I wrote the ballads at the beginning and the end, the scene in the basement of the school with the fire, the character of Srini, and the part where Freddy gets his good ear shot off. In fact, I wrote virtually all of the narration and dialogue and most of the jokes in the game; Al's design did not include more than a handful of sample jokes. He was working on Leisure Suit Larry 6 most of the time.

M.M.- Freddy is a rather linear game. To all the people out there claiming that good adventures should be non-linear, please can you tell what you acomplished with the linearity of that game?

J.M.- I think they're wrong. An adventure game, traditionally, tells a story. Well, stories are, and always have been, linear, and the invention of the computer has not changed storytelling. Good stories have a beginning, a middle, and an end, and are carefully constructed and paced so as to create dramatic tension, comic relief, excitement, and other emotions. You can't be sloppy or the story will not be very involving. Storytelling is not easy, and most people don't know how to tell stories well. Some people seem to be born storytellers, others must be trained.

In "non-linear" games, the player becomes the storyteller. Well, if the player is the storyteller, and if the player is not a VERY GOOD storyteller, then the player will not tell the story very well! You'll end up with a long, rambling game that does not build dramatic tension.

People say they want non-linear games, but that's not what they really want. When players are given non-linear adventures, they complain: "I didn't know what my goal was," "I didn't know where to go first or what to do." They really want direction. But they want it to be DISGUISED, so that they don't feel like it's OBVIOUS that they're being led around by the nose.

M.M.- There was quite some trouble with Space Quest 6: The Spinal Frontier. The early desing was done by you, but then you left Sierra in the midst of the production. Was the final game very different from your early plans?

Josh: The game was, in many ways, close to the original design. Scott did not change very much. He did change the closing, which I thought was not very dramatic, and he did not put in much text or dialogue. As a result, you can see the differences very clearly between the rooms I worked on and the rooms Scott worked on. The rooms I worked on had dozens and dozens of things you could click on and get a response. The rooms he worked on had very few responses. There were some puzzles implemented incorrectly because Scott either did not like, or did not understand, some of what I had designed. But the overall plot and movement of the story were very close to what I originally envisioned.

M.M.- What facets of the previous Space Quest chapters you wanted to avoid / retain in the sixth episode?

J.M.- One of the problems, I thought, with some of the earlier Space Quests was that the plots were not well-defined. Most of the time you just kind of rambled from situation to situation, and from planet to planet, without a clear idea of what your overall goal was, where you wanted to end up at the end of the game. I wanted to avoid that. I always wanted to avoid the fact that other Space Quests did not have a theme. By this, I mean a statement or attitude about humanity that becomes the "point" of the story, such as, "Life is about doing things for other people, not for yourself" or "Man's greatest inhumanity is to himself." I did not think it would hurt Space Quest to have a theme. After all, computer games have the power to influence people's moods and actions.

So I gave SQ6 the simple theme of "One cannot hope to make it through this world without friends." Roger never seemed, in other SQs, to have any real friends. So I wanted him to have Stellar Santiago as a friend (not as a love interest, he already had that with Beatrice), and for him to learn to value that friendship. I don't think that came through very well in the final game, because I don't think Scott understood that that's what I was designing.

What I really liked about the other SQs was the way they managed to satirize and parody so many other science-fiction games, TV shows, movies, and books. I think we did that better in SQ6 than in any of the other SQs. There are areas in SQ6 where nearly every single response is a joke, pun, or hidden reference to science fiction. I love that kind of thing!

M.M.- Later you joined Legend Entertainment. How did you like the change?

J.M.- It was a very difficult transition. At Legend, every single employee (except for the secretaries) was a programmer. So when I started designing, I was designing like I'd been doing at Sierra, and they weren't prepared to process that kind of design. They wanted me to design in "pseudocode," or in a kind of fake programming language, and I know very little about programming. We had to adjust to each other's expectations.

Also, the budgets at Legend were very small, and because the money was always coming from an outside investor, the budget would change during the course of the game's development. Things would go wrong and there wouldn't always be the money to fix them. Both Mike Verdu and I ended up paying for some things out of our own pockets so that they would be fixed.

Lastly, Legend was very, very small. I was employee #13. There were no artists on staff (we hired outside art companies), no composers. At Sierra, if you wanted to try something, you just found an artist who had a little free time, and they would help you. At Legend, you had to request it, and the request had to go to the art company, and they would charge you for it...it definitely hindered experimentation.

M.M.- At Legend, you designed the game Callahan's Crosstime Saloon. It is a shame that most Legend games are not usually published here in Spain, no matter how good they are or how much awards they have won. This game is based upon some short stories from author Spider Robinson. How does it feel writing a game based on other people's creations?

J.M.- It was not that different from many experiences at Sierra. After all, I worked on Space Quest (the creation of Mark Crowe and Scott Murphy), King's Quest and Laura Bow (Roberta's creations), and Leisure Suit Larry (Al's creation). Only Freddy Pharkas felt like it was partially my own creation.

Legend had given me a choice of writers to adapt, and I chose Spider Robinson because I loved and respected the Callahan's stories. Unlike Space Quest or King Quest or Leisure Suit Larry, Spider's stories had a very strong theme, and his characters were much more complex and "real" than someone like Roger or Larry, who are pretty much cartoon characters. Spider himself was wonderfully easy to work with; he knew that I respected the integrity of the books and would not have his characters doing things in the game that they would never do in the books. So he trusted me to do with them as I wanted. He only changed one thing: I had come up with an "origin" story for one of the characters, but Spider was already working on a book of origin stories (including one for that same character). So, at his request, I dropped the origin story and came up with something else.

What was especially gratifying was playing the game for Spider when it was finished. He sat with his mouth open, staring in amazement, and kept saying, "That's it! That's Callahan's Place!" That helped make it all worthwhile. And the fans of Callahan's had the same reaction. I felt very satisfied, if I could adapt the book into computer game form and NOT have people yelling at me that I'd ruined the books.

M.M.- As an exception, Legend's Blackstone Chronicles has been released in Spain. What do you think of this game? Can we expect new adventure games from Legend?

J.M.- I really enjoyed Blackstone Chronicles; the voice acting and the atmosphere were some of the best I've ever experienced in a computer game. Unfortunately, Legend has no more adventure games planned. They have been purchased by GT Interactive, and are now working on first-person or third-person "shooters" (maybe with some RPG elements). Specifically, they have Wheel of Time coming up, and the sequel to Unreal and an Unreal add-on. Not the kind of thing you usually associate with Legend!

M.M.- Your games tend to be very heavy on text, with tons of large descriptions filled with puns (that was the impression I got from Laura Bow II, Freddy Pharkas and Space Quest 6, and I believe that Callahan goes in the same direction). You do it that way to rettain the flavor of old Infocom text adventures, to mislead the players, as a funny way of hiding hints, just because you love writting,...?

J.M.- Well, yes, I love to write, but that's not really why I write such text-heavy games. Part of it is that I do love the Infocom adventures and I like the atmosphere I got from them. Part of it is that I love wordplay and jokes, and you can't do those in graphics (well, you can do "sight gags" with graphics, but graphics tend to be very expensive, so it's cheaper to write text jokes than to write visual jokes and then animate them). And part of it is that I don't like to rely just on the graphics to communicate all the little, funny details about the people and objects in the environments. The only way to flesh them out, since players don't have the benefit of touch, taste, and smell, is to describe them. Lastly, I don't do it to mislead players, but I do love to hide hints in the text.

That's one of the things that I think has almost vanished from adventure games: HINTS. I don't mean a hint system, I mean providing enough information to the player for them to solve puzzles without trial-and-error. Nowadays, you just click on everything until you find something that works. I would rather encourage people to read and observe and then THINK about what they've read and observed, and solve the puzzles that way. The trouble is, games like MYST have given people a different expectation, so they are no longer patient enough to go through the puzzle-solving process in a thoughtful way. They just want to get to the next pretty screen.

M.M.- When you sit down to create an adventure game, what are your main goals? How do you view the perfect adventure?

J.M.- My main goal is always to entertain. Some designers are so caught up in creating their own vision that they forget that games are a form of entertainment. You can't make people laugh or cry, or educate them, if you don't entertain them. If you don't entertain them, they will turn off the computer before you can get around to touching them on a deeper level.

The other goals change depending on the type of game I'm supposed to design. I try not to think about the perfect adventure. It makes my head hurt.

M.M.- Are you currently involved in the development of any adventure game?

J.M.- Not in the traditional sense. I am involved with the development of games at Sega that have some adventure game elements, but they have too many action game elements for me to think of them as adventure games. The market in the United States for adventure games has been extremely poor lately. so it's very difficult to get a publisher to agree to do an adventure.

M.M.- Sierra has been very controversial for the last years. Sierra grew and grew until it exploded. Do you feel there was a time were Sierra definitely went wrong?

J.M.- Yes, Sierra went wrong when it became a "public company," and started attracting outside investors. These outside investors cared more about the kind of huge growth you're talking about, and did not care about the content or quality of the games (except as they affected profits). Suddenly, people who knew nothing about games, and didn't WANT to know anything about games, were controlling the company's policies. Greed took over. I would've hoped that Sierra would be willing to stay small in exchange for retaining control over its content and quality. But Ken has always looked up to Bill Gates, and I think he wanted to create a huge empire.

M.M.- Comparing LucasArts and Sierra games, one difference wich is very clear is polishment: Lucas used to polish their games to the extent, while Sierra seemed a bit rushed releasing titles wich many times were somewhat unfinished. For a games company, what do you think is the best policy: holding on until the game is perfect [even going overbudget], or releasing games wich a few flaws?

J.M.- I think the hard part is judging the "point of diminishing returns," where you decide that the game is not perfect (no game ever is; they're too complicated), but it's close ENOUGH. At that point, you want to stop putting money into the game because the differences that additional money will make are too small to be noticed.

I think both companies have released some near-perfect games and some too-buggy games, and Sierra has released more of those than LucasArts. I'll say this in Sierra's defense, though: adventure games were what paid the bills at Sierra, and Sierra had to release enough of them, quickly enough, to keep the company going. LucasArts releases very few adventure games, not nearly enough to support the company. When you only release an adventure game every year or two, you can afford to take your time. Sierra did not have the luxury of perfection; they did not have the deep pockets of a George Lucas supporting them. Very few LucasArts adventure games were profitable; maybe they took too long polishing them.

M.M.- Is the destiny of any succesful, growing adventure game company to stop supporting adventure games in order to create other types of games more commercial and easier to produce?

J.M.- At the moment, this appears to be true. Legend, Sierra, LucasArts, Access...all of them have stopped producing adventure games because there is very little profit in that genre right now. But I think it would be wrong to say that that's the way it will always be.

M.M.- With commercial adventure games selling poorly, how do you view the future of adventure gaming? Would it be possible that those companys no longer interested in producing adventure games would find profit in distributing ones created by outside teams?

J.M.- That could be true, but I think the future is brighter than that. I think that sooner or later somebody will design an adventure game that helps revitalize the genre. Then all the companies that used to do adventures will start doing them again. The same thing happened with role-playing games. Five years ago, nobody in the US wanted to make RPGs. Now they're popular again. It works in a cycle, eventually games that are out of fashion come back into fashion.

M.M.- I know this question is not very original and reminds to the most boring usenet threads but... if you were left in a deserted island, and you had to choose three adventure games to accompany you, the winners would be...

J.M.- Yowch, this is tough! I would want to choose games that I never finished, not games I had already played and loved. So I think I would choose:

A. A Mind Forever Voyaging
B. Gabriel Knight 3 (I know it's not out yet!)
C. Suspended

And this way, even if my 3-D graphics card overheats, I still have two games to play!

M.M.- If there is anything else you would like to tell us, feel free to do it.

J.M.- I'm all typed out! But I thank you kindly for the chance to talk to Spanish adventure game players, and I can only hope I get to do an adventure game soon, hopefully one that's available in Spain!

M.M.- Many thanks for your fabulous answers, Josh. Good luck and happy adventuring!
